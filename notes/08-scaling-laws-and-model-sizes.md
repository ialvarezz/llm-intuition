# 08 · Scaling Laws & Model Sizes

## The curves: loss as a predictable power law

The single biggest reason labs are comfortable spending hundreds of millions of dollars training a model before they've seen it work is that they don't have to guess how good it'll be. Kaplan et al.'s scaling laws paper found that language model loss "scales as a power-law with model size, dataset size, and the amount of compute used for training, with some trends spanning more than seven orders of magnitude" [S1]. That's an unusually strong empirical regularity for a field this young — plot loss against any of those three quantities on a log-log chart, and instead of noisy scatter you get a nearly straight line. The same paper adds a detail that's easy to miss but matters for how curves get read: "Other architectural details such as network width or depth have minimal effects within a wide range" [S1] — the shape of the network barely moves the needle; scale does almost all the work.

That predictability is what turns scaling from a guess into a bet you can size. If loss on a small, cheap run falls on the curve you expect, you can extrapolate that same curve out to a run 1,000x larger and have real confidence in roughly where the loss will land — before spending the money. Kaplan et al. go further and use the curves prescriptively: "These relationships allow us to determine the optimal allocation of a fixed compute budget." The paper concludes that "Larger models are significantly more sample-efficient, such that optimally compute-efficient training involves training very large models on a relatively modest amount of data and stopping significantly before convergence" [S1]. That specific recommendation — bigger model, comparatively less data, stop early — became the operating assumption behind a wave of very large, comparatively undertrained models. It's also the exact assumption Chinchilla overturned two years later.

## Chinchilla's correction: undertrained, not just small

Hoffmann et al.'s Chinchilla paper revisits the same question — optimal model size and token count for a fixed compute budget — and reaches a different prescription. Its framing is blunt: "current large language models are significantly undertrained, a consequence of the recent focus on scaling language models whilst keeping the amount of training data constant" [S2]. Labs had been following the bigger-model-don't-worry-as-much-about-data reading of the original scaling-laws work, and Chinchilla's authors argue that left a lot of quality on the table. By training over 400 models "ranging from 70 million to over 16 billion parameters on 5 to 500 billion tokens" [S2], they arrived at a cleaner rule: "for compute-optimal training, the model size and the number of training tokens should be scaled equally: for every doubling of model size the number of training tokens should also be doubled" [S2]. Model size and data aren't independent knobs to trade off against each other arbitrarily — for a given compute budget, there's a specific ratio that gets the most loss reduction per dollar, and it isn't simply making the model as big as possible.

The paper backs this with an actual model, not just a formula. Chinchilla uses the same compute budget as Gopher but with 70B parameters and, per the abstract, four times as much training data [S2] — same training cost as a 280B-parameter model, spent instead on a 70B model trained roughly four times longer. Put a number on that ratio: the paper's Table 1 lists Chinchilla at 70 billion parameters trained on 1.4 trillion tokens [S2] — 1.4 trillion divided by 70 billion works out to about 20 tokens per parameter, the rough compute-optimal heuristic that came out of this paper and that people still reach for as a first-pass sizing rule. The result: Chinchilla "uniformly and significantly outperforms Gopher (280B), GPT-3 (175B), Jurassic-1 (178B), and Megatron-Turing NLG (530B) on a large range of downstream evaluation tasks" [S2] — a model a quarter the size of Gopher, and less than a third the size of GPT-3, beating both. That's the concrete version of a smaller, longer-trained model winning: a compute-optimal 70B model outperforming a 175B model that was trained on the older, parameter-heavy recipe — 175B is 2.5x Chinchilla's 70B parameter count. Chinchilla also reported "a state-of-the-art average accuracy of 67.5% on the MMLU benchmark, greater than a 7% improvement over Gopher" [S2] — the win wasn't marginal. There's a practical bonus baked into the same result: a smaller model that matches or beats a larger one is also cheaper to run afterward — the paper notes Chinchilla "uses substantially less compute for fine-tuning and inference, greatly facilitating downstream usage" [S2]. Get the training ratio right and you save money twice: once during training, and again on every inference call for the model's whole deployed lifetime.

## Emergent-looking capabilities

Scaling laws describe loss — a smooth, continuous number that a model optimizes directly. What labs and users actually care about is usually something else: can the model do multi-step arithmetic, follow a chain of instructions, write working code. Wei et al.'s "Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models" paper is about the gap between those two things. Loss itself scales predictably — Wei et al. frame that as the established baseline, noting "Scaling up language models has been shown to predictably improve performance and sample efficiency on a wide range of downstream tasks" [S3] — but the paper's actual subject is the opposite pattern on specific tasks: "an unpredictable phenomenon that we refer to as emergent abilities of large language models. We consider an ability to be emergent if it is not present in smaller models but is present in larger models. Thus, emergent abilities cannot be predicted simply by extrapolating the performance of smaller models" [S3].

In practice this shows up as a task where a whole run of smaller models sit near random-chance accuracy, and then, at some model size, accuracy jumps — not gradually, but as something closer to a step function on that particular benchmark. It's a genuinely different shape from the smooth loss curve underneath it, which is exactly the paper's point: a smoothly improving loss can still produce discontinuous-looking jumps on discrete downstream tasks, because task accuracy is a much coarser, thresholded measurement of the same underlying improvement. The paper's closing implication is why this matters to anyone betting on the next scale-up: "The existence of such emergence implies that additional scaling could further expand the range of capabilities of language models" [S3] — some of what the next size tier can do may not be visible at all in the current one, a very different kind of bet than extrapolating a smooth loss curve.

## Why labs bet billions on curves

Put the last two sections together and you get the actual economic logic behind frontier training runs. The loss curve (S1) is what makes a nine- or ten-figure training run underwritable at all — you can point to a curve fit on cheaper, smaller runs and defend, with real evidence, roughly what loss the expensive run will land at before it starts. Chinchilla (S2) is what makes that bet efficient rather than wasteful — it sets the ratio between parameters and training tokens so the compute budget isn't spent on a model that's too big for the data it saw. And the emergence question (S3) is the reason the bet is worth making at all beyond a moderate size: if some capabilities only show up once a model crosses a size threshold, the only way to find out what a bigger model can do is to actually build it, and no amount of loss extrapolation on smaller runs tells you in advance which capabilities cross that threshold next.

## The size spectrum in practice

None of this means every deployed model should be as large as possible. In practice, models ship across a size spectrum — small, fast, cheap models at one end, and frontier-scale models at the other — and picking a point on that spectrum is a real engineering decision, not just a budget constraint. Anthropic's own model lineup makes the trade-off explicit: multiple models are sold side by side, described in terms of the same three axes — cost, speed, and capability — rather than as one "best" model, and the docs frame the choice as a live decision: "If you're unsure which model to use, start with" a specific recommended model, with a separate note for "workloads that need the highest available capability" [S4]. A small model is cheaper per token and responds faster; a frontier model is more capable but costs more and takes longer per response. Distillation is the technique that lets a smaller model inherit some of a larger one's quality — training a compact model to imitate a bigger model's outputs — which is part of how "fast and cheap" models manage to stay reasonably capable instead of falling straight back to an older, weaker generation.

Inference cost scales with model size in a way training cost doesn't get the chance to: a training run happens once, but every single API call to a deployed model burns compute proportional to that model's size, for as long as it's in production. A model that's 10x the size of another isn't just 10x the training cost — it's roughly 10x the cost of every response it ever generates, for millions or billions of calls over its deployment lifetime. That's why model choice is an engineering question with real trade-offs on both sides, not a solved default of always picking the biggest one available.

## Pro vs. amateur

**Amateurs think bigger is better is still the whole story. Pros know that stopped being true in 2022.** Chinchilla's entire contribution was showing that a large fraction of pre-2022 models were undertrained relative to their size [S2] — parameter count alone was never the metric that predicted quality, compute-optimal *ratio* of parameters to training data was. Post-Chinchilla, data quality and post-training (see note 07) do at least as much work as raw parameter count in determining how good a model actually feels to use.

**Amateurs treat parameter count as a reliable proxy for quality across model families. Pros know it's a weak signal outside a single, consistent training recipe.** Two models with the same parameter count, trained by different labs on different data with different post-training pipelines, can differ enormously in quality — and a well-trained smaller model can beat an older, larger one, which is precisely the shape of Chinchilla's own headline result against Gopher and GPT-3 [S2]. Parameter count only compares cleanly within one family trained the same way; across families it tells you almost nothing on its own.

**Amateurs default to the biggest available model for every task. Pros treat latency, cost, and quality as a three-way trade to route deliberately.** Because inference cost and latency scale with model size, and because production model lineups are explicitly built and priced across a size spectrum rather than as one model [S4], the efficient move is routing easy, high-volume tasks (classification, extraction, short replies) to small, fast models and reserving frontier-scale models for the requests that actually need the extra capability — not running every request through the largest model by default.

**Amateurs trust leaderboard rankings as a ground truth. Pros know benchmarks saturate and get gamed, and trust task-specific evals instead.** A model's position on a general leaderboard says less than it looks like it says once a benchmark is old enough to have training data contaminated by it, or narrow enough to be optimized against directly. The only evaluation that reliably predicts how a model will perform on *your* task is an eval built from your task's own data and failure modes — general benchmarks are a starting filter, not a substitute for that.
