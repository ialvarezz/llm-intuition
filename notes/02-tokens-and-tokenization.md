# 02 · Tokens & tokenization

## The unit the model actually sees

An LLM never sees words, sentences, or characters. It sees **tokens** — the smallest individual units a language model operates on, and they "can correspond to words, subwords, characters, or even bytes" depending on the tokenizer [S6]. Before any text reaches the network, a separate step called tokenization converts your string into a sequence of integers, each one an index into a fixed vocabulary the model was trained with. Everything downstream — embeddings, attention, the final probability distribution over "what comes next" (note 01) — operates on that integer sequence, not on the text you typed.

This is not a minor implementation detail you can safely ignore. Two things that look identical to you as a human reader — "hello" and " hello" — can be different tokens with different IDs, different embeddings, and different learned behavior, because the tokenizer, not the model, decided where the boundaries go before training ever started [S9].

## Why not characters

The most obvious scheme would be to feed the model one character at a time. It would work — you'd never see an "unknown character" error, and the vocabulary would be tiny (about 256 possible byte values). But it fails on a different axis: length. A paragraph that's 40 tokens under a subword scheme might be 200+ characters under a character scheme. Since a transformer's cost and its usable context both scale with sequence length (note 05 covers context windows), turning every word into 4-8 separate steps multiplies the effective cost of every prompt and burns through the model's working memory that much faster for the same amount of actual content.

## Why not whole words

The opposite extreme — one token per whole word — has the reverse problem: vocabulary explosion. English alone has hundreds of thousands of word forms once you count plurals, tenses, and compounds; add code identifiers, misspellings, product names, and non-English text, and a whole-word vocabulary would need to be enormous or it will constantly hit words it has never seen. A fixed vocabulary can't contain "every string a user might ever type" — this is precisely the problem subword segmentation was built to solve. The original subword-unit paper frames it directly: "Neural machine translation (NMT) models typically operate with a fixed vocabulary, but translation is an open-vocabulary problem" [S4]. Rare and unknown words break a whole-word vocabulary in a way that a subword vocabulary, by construction, cannot.

## Subwords: the middle ground

Tokenizers used by essentially every modern LLM split text into **subword fragments** — pieces smaller than most words but larger than single characters — drawn from a fixed vocabulary that's typically somewhere in the 50,000 to 200,000 range. `tiktoken`, OpenAI's tokenizer library, documents encodings such as `o200k_base` and `cl100k_base` used across different model families [S3], and Karpathy's own reference implementation walks through building tokenizers "with adjustable vocabulary sizes," in examples ranging "from 4,096 up to 100K tokens" [S5]. Common whole words ("the", "is") often end up as a single token because they're frequent enough to earn their own slot; rare or compound words get split into two or more familiar pieces ("token" + "ization"); truly novel strings — a username, a typo, a string of digits — fall all the way back to individual bytes or characters, which is always a fallback path this scheme guarantees, unlike a closed whole-word vocabulary.

## How BPE carves text

The dominant algorithm behind this middle ground is **byte pair encoding (BPE)**, adapted from a data-compression technique into a vocabulary-building tool by the paper that introduced it for translation [S4]. The construction process:

1. **Start from raw bytes.** A real byte-level BPE tokenizer's base vocabulary is the 256 possible byte values — "Tokens initially go from 0 to 255... because we start with UTF-8 encoded bytes, and each byte can hold values from 0 to 255" [S1]. This guarantees any string, in any language, in any encoding, can always be represented — there's no "out of vocabulary" case, because you can always fall back to raw bytes.
2. **Count adjacent pairs.** Across a huge training corpus, count how often every pair of adjacent tokens (initially, adjacent bytes) occurs.
3. **Merge the most frequent pair.** Whatever pair occurs most often gets combined into a new, single token, added to the vocabulary.
4. **Repeat.** Re-count, re-merge, over and over — "iteratively identify pairs... that appear the most often, define new tokens based on those, and replace them in the string... repeatedly merges the most frequent adjacent pair to create new tokens" [S1] — until the vocabulary reaches its target size (the ~50k-200k range mentioned above).

This is a purely statistical, data-driven process. No one hand-writes the rule "'ing' should be a token." It becomes one because the training corpus contains the byte sequence for "ing" often enough, adjacent to enough other things, that repeatedly merging the most frequent pair eventually produces it. `tiktoken`'s own description of the result captures the intent: BPE is "reversible and lossless," "works on arbitrary text," "compresses text" (each resulting token averages roughly 4 bytes), and "helps models recognize common subwords like 'ing' for better generalization" [S3]. Production tokenizers add one more wrinkle before merging even starts: GPT-2's tokenizer "applies a preprocessing chunking step using regex that segments text and runs BPE only within chunks to prevent undesirable merges such as joining words to punctuation" [S1] — so, for instance, a word and the comma after it are kept from merging into a single ugly token.

## One text, many tokenizations

Because the vocabulary and merge rules are learned from *a specific training corpus*, different model families end up with different tokenizers, and the same input string can tokenize to a different number of tokens depending on which one processes it. This isn't a hypothetical — it's directly measurable. Anthropic's own documentation states that its newest tokenizer, introduced with a recent model generation, "produces approximately 30% more tokens than on earlier models" for the same input text, and explicitly warns against "reusing counts measured against earlier models" when estimating cost or context fit on a new one [S7]. `tiktoken` similarly ships multiple named encodings (`o200k_base`, `cl100k_base`, and per-model mappings via `encoding_for_model()`) precisely because different model generations use different vocabularies [S3]. If you've ever had a prompt that "fit" under one model's context limit suddenly overflow after switching models, this is very often why: you didn't change your text, you changed the tokenizer counting it.

## Special tokens

Beyond the tokens learned from ordinary text, a production vocabulary reserves a handful of **special tokens** that never appear in raw input — they're inserted programmatically to mark structure the model needs to recognize. The most universal are BOS (beginning-of-sequence) and EOS (end-of-sequence) markers, which tell the model where a document starts and stops. Chat-tuned models add further special tokens to mark conversational structure — role boundaries like "this is where the user's turn starts" and "this is where the assistant's turn starts" — the chat-format scaffolding that turns a raw next-token predictor into something that can hold a multi-turn conversation (note 01 covers the base-model-to-assistant transition this scaffolding supports). These tokens live in the same vocabulary space as ordinary subwords but are never produced by the BPE merge process on user text; they're reserved IDs the tokenizer inserts at fixed points.

## Pro vs. amateur

**Amateurs budget in words. Pros budget in tokens.** Context windows and API pricing are both denominated in tokens, not words [S7][S2]. For English prose, OpenAI's developer docs state the standard rule of thumb: "1 token is approximately 4 characters or 0.75 words for English text" — about 1.3 tokens per word [S8]. That ratio holds loosely for plain English, but it silently breaks down for code and non-Latin scripts, which tend to tokenize far less efficiently per character because the training data that shaped the vocabulary's merges skewed toward common English patterns. An amateur estimates "my prompt is 2,000 words, the context window is 8,000 tokens, plenty of room" and gets a length error; a pro knows that estimate needs a real tokenizer count, especially for anything that isn't plain English prose.

**Amateurs assume a text has one true token count. Pros know it depends on which tokenizer is counting.** The same string produces a different token count on different model families, and even across generations of the same vendor's models — Anthropic's docs are explicit that a newer tokenizer "produces approximately 30% more tokens than on earlier models" for identical input, and that you should "Recount prompts against the model you plan to use" rather than reuse an old count [S7]. Treating a token count as a fixed property of a string, rather than a property of (string, tokenizer) together, is the single most common source of "why did my context suddenly overflow after I switched models."

**Amateurs are baffled by weird model behavior on rare strings. Pros recognize it as a tokenization artifact.** Usernames, long numbers, unusual product names, and rare non-English words often don't have a dedicated token — they get fragmented into an unfamiliar sequence of small byte- or character-level pieces the model saw far less often during training. That fragmentation is frequently the real explanation behind a model mishandling a specific string, garbling a number, or behaving inconsistently on what looks to a human like a simple, ordinary piece of text.

**Amateurs treat leading whitespace as cosmetic. Pros know it can change the token.** Because tokenizers are trained on raw byte sequences, " hello" (with a leading space) and "hello" (without one) are frequently different tokens with different IDs and different learned associations, not the same token with a space glued on separately [S9]. This matters directly for prompt construction: how you concatenate strings — whether a trailing space or newline creeps in before the next piece of text — can shift which tokens actually get produced, even though the visible text looks the same either way.

**Amateurs expect the model to spell and count reliably. Pros know why it can't.** A model never sees "s-t-r-a-w-b-e-r-r-y" as eight individual letters; it sees whatever subword chunks the tokenizer happened to split that word into, which is rarely one-token-per-letter. Asking it to count the letters in a word, or reverse a string character by character, is asking it to reason about a representation (characters) it structurally never had direct access to during training — it can only approximate the answer statistically from patterns in text that talk about spelling, not by actually iterating over characters the way code would (note 01 touches this same limitation from the introspection angle).
