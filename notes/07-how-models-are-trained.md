# 07 · How Models Are Trained

## Pretraining: growing an autocomplete engine

Before a model can be an assistant, it has to become something much dumber and much more general: a next-token predictor. Pretraining takes a raw architecture and trillions of tokens of text — web pages, books, code, forum posts — and trains it on a single objective, over and over: given everything so far, predict the next token. Nothing about *being helpful* is in that objective. Nothing about answering questions, following instructions, or refusing bad requests is in it either. The only signal is: did you guess the next token correctly.

What's striking is how much falls out of that one objective, given enough scale. To get good at predicting the next token across a huge, varied corpus, the model has to implicitly learn grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, code syntax, argument structure — because all of that shows up in the statistics of what token comes next. Loss goes down over the course of pretraining, and as it does, capabilities that were never directly trained for start to emerge as side effects of getting better at the underlying prediction task. This is the stage that costs the most, by a wide margin — Meta describes producing Llama 3 as building "our dataset for language model pre-training from a variety of data sources containing knowledge until the end of 2023" [S3], a dataset assembled and locked in well before any later fine-tuning happens.

The thing that comes out the other end of pretraining is usually called a *base model*, and it's important to be precise about what that is: a very good, very general document-completion engine, not an assistant. Feed it *The capital of France is* and it will complete the sentence. Feed it a question, and depending on what similar text looked like in its training data, it might answer, might continue the question with more questions, might drift into a forum-post-style tangent, or might just keep generating similar-looking text without ever answering in the way a chatbot would. A base model has no built-in concept of being the assistant half of a conversation — that framing has to be taught later. Two independent write-ups of Karpathy's "State of GPT" talk (a widely cited walkthrough of this exact pipeline) converge on the same paraphrased characterization: base models are trained to complete documents, not to act as assistants [S4][S5]. Two independent summaries of the same talk landing on the same framing is a useful signal that this is a stable, well-known characterization of what pretraining actually produces — see the References section for why the talk itself isn't quoted directly here.

## Supervised fine-tuning (SFT): teaching the format

A base model can technically produce a correct answer to *explain what a token is*, but it has no learned habit of doing so cleanly, and no reason to prefer that over rambling into related trivia. Supervised fine-tuning fixes that by training the model on a much smaller, much higher-quality dataset: thousands of curated (instruction → good response) pairs, written or approved by human labelers, that demonstrate what a well-formed helpful answer actually looks like. Where pretraining predicts the next token over a slice of the internet, SFT predicts the next token over a curated set of examples that all look like a helpful assistant talking.

The InstructGPT paper — the source that popularized this exact pipeline for the models most people now use — describes this first step plainly: "Our labelers provide demonstrations of the desired behavior on the input prompt distribution", and that demonstration dataset is what the base model gets fine-tuned on using ordinary supervised learning [S1]. It's a much smaller, much cheaper stage than pretraining, but it's the one that does the most obvious work of turning a document-completer into something that looks and acts like a chat assistant: answering the question that was actually asked, in a recognizable format, instead of wandering off into whatever a similar-looking passage on the internet would have done next.

## RLHF: optimizing toward what people actually prefer

SFT teaches the model to imitate good responses, but imitation caps out — the model can only be as good as the examples it was shown, and there's no mechanism in SFT for the model to improve past what a human demonstrator happened to write down. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) adds a second, different kind of signal on top: instead of imitating one fixed *correct* answer, the model learns to produce whichever answer humans *prefer* out of several candidates, even when no human ever wrote that exact answer down.

The mechanism, per InstructGPT, runs in two more steps after SFT. First: "We collect a dataset of comparisons between model outputs, where labelers indicate which output they prefer", and that comparison data is used to train a separate reward model — a model whose entire job is to look at a response and predict how much a human labeler would like it [S1]. Second, the original (SFT) model gets optimized against that reward model: "We use the output of the RM as a scalar reward. We fine-tune the supervised policy to optimize this reward using the PPO algorithm" [S1]. The result is a model that's been pushed, response by response, in the direction of whatever the reward model believes humans rank highest — and the payoff was large: InstructGPT's paper reports that "labelers significantly prefer InstructGPT outputs across model sizes", with a 1.3-billion-parameter InstructGPT model beating outputs from the 175-billion-parameter base GPT-3 in human evaluations, despite having roughly 100x fewer parameters [S1].

## Constitutional AI / RLAIF: Anthropic's variant

RLHF as described above needs a constant stream of human labelers ranking outputs, which is slow and expensive, and asks people to personally read and judge harmful or upsetting content in order to train a model away from producing it. Anthropic's Constitutional AI paper proposes a variant that keeps the same basic two-phase shape — a supervised phase, then a preference-optimization phase — but replaces the human ranking step with AI-generated feedback guided by a written set of principles (the "constitution").

In the supervised phase, the paper describes generating a response, then having the model critique its own response against a stated principle, then fine-tuning on the revised response: "we first generate responses to harmfulness prompts using a helpful-only AI assistant... We then ask the model to critique its response according to a principle in the constitution, and then revise the original response in light of the critique" [S2]. In the RL phase, instead of humans ranking two candidate responses, another model does: "This stage mimics RLHF, except that we replace human preferences for harmlessness with 'AI feedback'" — the paper calls this "RL from AI Feedback" (RLAIF) [S2]. The paper is explicit that the goal isn't to avoid RLHF outright but to reduce dependence on it: Constitutional AI "improves upon, and partially replaces reinforcement learning from human feedback" by relying on natural-language principles instead of the "tens of thousands of human preference labels" that pure RLHF typically requires [S2]. It's the same shape of stage — sample candidate outputs, rank them, train a preference model, optimize against it — just with the ranking signal generated by an AI model following written rules instead of by a human rater directly.

## Why each stage exists

It's worth being explicit about why this is three (or four) separate stages instead of one, because each stage is solving a genuinely different problem:

- **Pretraining builds capability.** This is where the model acquires knowledge, language ability, reasoning patterns, and world model — everything it could possibly say, correct or not, comes from here. Nothing later adds new capability of this kind; later stages only reshape how the existing capability gets expressed.
- **SFT teaches format.** The base model already *knows* a lot, in the sense that the information is somewhere in its weights, but it has no habit of presenting that knowledge as a clean, on-topic, assistant-style answer. SFT installs that habit by direct demonstration.
- **RLHF (or RLAIF) aligns toward preference.** Format alone doesn't guarantee the response people actually want — a technically well-formatted answer can still be unhelpful, evasive, rude, or the wrong length. RLHF pushes the model toward whatever real evaluators (human or AI, guided by principles) consistently prefer, using a reward signal that can generalize past the specific examples any human wrote by hand.

Put together, this is also where a model's *personality* comes from — not from pretraining, which just produces raw capability, but from the two post-training stages layered on top. The tone, the hedging habits, the refusal patterns, the level of formality, whether it opens with a summary or dives straight into detail — all of that is a product of what the SFT examples looked like and what the reward model was trained to prefer, not of some property inherent to the model as a mathematical object. Two different labs post-training the exact same base model with different SFT data and different reward signals would end up with two assistants that feel meaningfully different to talk to.

## Pro vs. amateur

**Amateurs assume a model's knowledge cutoff tracks its release or deployment date. Pros know it tracks the pretraining data.** A model announced or deployed in a given month can still have a knowledge cutoff many months earlier, because the pretraining corpus was assembled, filtered, and frozen well before training even started, and none of the post-training stages add new world knowledge at any real scale. Meta's own description of Llama 3's pretraining says the dataset contains "knowledge until the end of 2023" [S3] — that boundary is set at the data-collection stage, long before the model that eventually ships to users. If a model seems unaware of something recent, that's a pretraining-corpus fact, not a bug in whatever came after.

**Amateurs think a model refuses or over-apologizes because it *understood* the request was bad. Pros know refusal behavior is a trained preference, not comprehension.** Refusals, hedging, and excessive caveats aren't the model reasoning its way to caution — they're patterns that got reinforced because a reward model (trained on human or AI preference data) consistently scored that kind of response higher for that kind of prompt. That's exactly the mechanism both InstructGPT's PPO-against-a-reward-model step [S1] and Constitutional AI's RLAIF step [S2] are built around: optimize the policy toward whatever the reward signal rewards. A model that over-refuses isn't being extra thoughtful; it's exhibiting a training artifact, and different post-training choices produce visibly different refusal behavior on the same underlying base model.

**Amateurs try to teach a model new facts by fine-tuning it. Pros know fine-tuning shapes behavior, not knowledge.** SFT and RLHF/RLAIF are about *how* the model expresses what it already has some representation of — format, tone, preference-alignment — not about injecting new, reliable factual content. Small fine-tuning datasets (thousands of examples, per InstructGPT's SFT step [S1]) are nowhere near the scale of pretraining's trillions of tokens, so they're a poor tool for reliably teaching a model new facts; the model may memorize a fact from a fine-tuning set inconsistently, or not at all, or generalize it incorrectly. That's precisely the gap retrieval-augmented approaches exist to fill — put the facts in context at inference time instead of trying to bake them into weights after the fact.

**Amateurs think the assistant persona is the whole model. Pros know a base model is still in there, underneath.** SFT and RLHF reshape how the model responds, but they don't erase the underlying next-token-prediction machinery pretraining built — they bias it. Push a deployed model with an unusual prompt structure, a broken conversation format, or an edge case the post-training data didn't cover well, and it can lapse back toward raw-autocomplete behavior: rambling, drifting off-topic, completing a pattern instead of answering a question. That's the base model's document-completion habit showing through the assistant veneer, not a new failure mode appearing from nowhere — independent coverage of Karpathy's talk on this exact pipeline makes the point that base models are trained to complete documents, not to be assistants, and that framing doesn't fully disappear just because later stages taught the model to usually act otherwise [S4][S5].
