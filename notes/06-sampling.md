# 06 · Sampling

## Logits are not an answer

Run a prompt through a language model and the last thing that happens, before any text comes out, is a single matrix multiplication that produces one raw score — a *logit* — for every token in the model's vocabulary, all fifty-thousand-plus of them, all at once. A logit is not a probability. It's an unbounded real number: could be 6.0, could be -3.4, could be 40. Higher means the model thinks that token is a more plausible continuation; that's the only thing a logit tells you on its own. To turn that pile of raw scores into something you can actually pick from, the model runs them through softmax — the function that exponentiates every score and divides each by the sum of all the exponentials, so the results are all positive and sum to exactly 1. What comes out the other side is a genuine probability distribution over the entire vocabulary: one number per token, all non-negative, all adding up to 100%.

That distribution is where the actual decision happens, and it's worth sitting with how different this is from what it *feels* like to use a chat model. It feels like the model "decides" what to say next. It doesn't. It scores every possible next token, all in one shot, and then a completely separate step — a decoding strategy — has to pick one. The model's job ends at the probability distribution. Everything after that, greedy decoding, temperature, top-p, top-k, is not the model "thinking," it's an algorithm rolling dice against a distribution the model handed it. Different dice-rolling rules produce visibly different text from the *exact same* model and the *exact same* logits. Holtzman et al.'s paper on nucleus sampling makes this point directly: "decoding strategies alone can dramatically effect the quality of machine text, even when generated from exactly the same neural language model" [S1]. The model is not the whole story. The sampler is a second, independent lever.

## Greedy: always pick the winner, and regret it

The simplest possible decoding strategy is greedy search: at every step, just take the single highest-probability token. Hugging Face's guide to decoding methods defines it exactly this way — greedy search "selects the word with the highest probability as its next word... at each timestep" [S2]. No randomness, no dice roll, just argmax over and over.

It sounds like the obviously correct choice — why wouldn't you want the model's single best guess every time? — but in practice it produces text that reads as flat and repetitive. The nucleus sampling paper diagnoses exactly why: "using likelihood as a decoding objective leads to text that is bland and strangely repetitive" [S1]. The intuition is that always taking the top pick locks the model into a kind of rut. Once a phrase starts down a well-trodden statistical path, greedy decoding has no mechanism to ever deviate from the most likely continuation, so it tends to loop back into the same safe phrases and even literal repeated substrings. It's the model playing it maximally safe at every single step, and maximally safe, applied token after token after token, reads as robotic. This is also why greedy decoding is fully deterministic: same prompt in, same output out, every time, because there's no randomness anywhere in the process.

## Temperature: turning up (or down) the randomness

Temperature is the first real knob on the sampler, and it works by reshaping the probability distribution *before* anything gets sampled from it. Concretely, temperature divides every logit by a value `T` before the softmax runs. Hugging Face frames the mechanism plainly: the trick is to make the distribution "sharper (increasing the likelihood of high probability words and decreasing the likelihood of low probability words) by lowering the so-called `temperature` of the softmax" [S2].

Run that logic in both directions and the two extremes make intuitive sense. A low temperature (say 0.1) divides every logit by a small number, which stretches the gaps between scores apart before softmax — the current top-scoring token ends up capturing almost all of the probability mass, and sampling from that distribution looks almost exactly like greedy decoding. A high temperature (say 2.0) divides logits by a large number, which compresses the gaps between scores toward zero — every token's score gets pulled closer together, the distribution flattens toward uniform, and even a token that started out with a mediocre logit gets a real shot at being picked. Anthropic's own API documentation states this as a direct usage recommendation: "use `temperature` closer to `0.0` for analytical / multiple choice, and closer to `1.0` for creative and generative tasks" [S3]. Low temperature narrows the field toward the model's most confident pick; high temperature widens it toward genuine diversity — at the cost of more of that diversity being nonsense.

## Top-p (nucleus sampling): truncate the tail, then sample

Temperature reshapes the whole distribution but never removes any token from consideration — a wildly implausible token still has *some* nonzero chance of getting picked at a high enough temperature, however small. Top-p, also called nucleus sampling, works differently: it truncates the distribution down to a much smaller candidate set before sampling ever happens.

The mechanism, per the Hugging Face guide: "Top-p sampling chooses from the smallest possible set of words whose cumulative probability exceeds the probability p. The probability mass is then redistributed among this set of words" [S2]. Concretely: sort every token by probability from highest to lowest, walk down that list adding up probabilities, and stop the instant the running total crosses `p`. Everything in that stopped-at set is the "nucleus" — the only tokens eligible to be sampled — and everything below the cutoff is discarded entirely, however large the tail of remaining tokens might be. Anthropic's docs describe the identical mechanism from the API side: "we compute the cumulative distribution over all the options for each subsequent token in decreasing probability order and cut it off once it reaches a particular probability specified by `top_p`" [S3].

This is exactly the fix the nucleus sampling paper was built around. Its abstract describes the goal as "sampling text from the dynamic nucleus of the probability distribution, which allows for diversity while effectively truncating the less reliable tail of the distribution" [S1]. The word *dynamic* matters: unlike top-k (below), the nucleus doesn't have a fixed size. If the model is very confident, the top handful of tokens alone might already exceed `p`, so the nucleus is small. If the model is uncertain and probability is spread thin across many plausible tokens, the nucleus has to include more of them to reach the same cumulative threshold. Top-p adapts to how confident the model actually is at each step; a fixed cutoff can't do that.

## Top-k: the simpler, blunter cousin

Top-k is the older, cruder version of the same idea: instead of cutting off at a cumulative probability threshold, it just keeps a fixed number of the most likely tokens and discards the rest, whatever their combined probability happens to add up to. Hugging Face: "the K most likely next words are filtered and the probability mass is redistributed among only those K next words" [S2]. Anthropic's docs frame the purpose the same way — top-k is "used to remove 'long tail' low probability responses" [S3].

The weakness top-p was designed to fix is exactly top-k's fixed size. Set `k = 40` and you always sample from 40 candidates, whether the model was extremely confident (in which case most of those 40 are near-zero-probability noise you didn't need to include) or extremely uncertain (in which case 40 tokens might not even cover half the real probability mass). Top-p's cumulative-probability rule adapts to that uncertainty automatically; top-k just doesn't. That's also why both API providers flag it as a secondary control — Anthropic marks top_k "recommended for advanced use cases only" [S3], the same caveat it gives top_p — most usage in practice tunes temperature and, if anything, top-p, and leaves top-k alone.

## Stop sequences and max-tokens: how generation ends

None of this matters if generation never stops, so two more parameters bound the output on the way out rather than reshaping the distribution on the way in. `max_tokens` is a hard ceiling — Anthropic's docs describe it as "the maximum number of tokens to generate before stopping," noting the model "may stop before reaching this maximum" since it's an upper bound, not a target [S3]. `stop_sequences` is a custom trigger: specific strings that, the moment the model emits them, immediately end generation. Anthropic's docs note the model "will normally stop when they have naturally completed their turn" on its own, but stop sequences give you a way to force an earlier cutoff — useful for things like stopping generation the instant the model starts a new turn marker or a section header. OpenAI's chat completion parameters describe the equivalent feature as "sequences where the API will stop generating further tokens," noting explicitly that "the returned text will not contain the stop sequence" itself [S4]. Both together are the practical bookends of any generation call — the sampler decides *what* to write, `max_tokens` and `stop_sequences` decide *when to stop writing it*.

## Pro vs. amateur

**Amateurs assume temperature 0 means fully deterministic output. Pros know it doesn't, in production.** Anthropic's own API docs say it directly: "even with `temperature` of `0.0`, the results will not be fully deterministic" [S3]. Even at the theoretical floor of randomness, floating-point arithmetic and request batching on real GPU hardware introduce tiny nondeterminism that can occasionally flip the argmax between two nearly-tied logits. If you need byte-for-byte reproducible output for testing or auditing, temperature 0 gets you close, not guaranteed — plan around that, don't assume it away.

**Amateurs crank both temperature and top-p at once to "get more variety." Pros tune one, not both blindly.** Both parameters push in the same direction — more randomness — and stacking them compounds unpredictably rather than adding cleanly. Both major providers say so explicitly in their own parameter docs: OpenAI's temperature docstring reads "We generally recommend altering this or `top_p` but not both," and the top_p docstring mirrors it back: "We generally recommend altering this or `temperature` but not both" [S4]. Pick the knob that maps to what you're actually trying to control — temperature for how sharp vs. flat the whole distribution is, top-p for how large the eligible candidate set is — and leave the other at its default.

**Amateurs treat "more creative" as strictly better. Pros know high temperature buys diversity at the direct cost of hallucination risk.** Cranking temperature up doesn't just make phrasing more varied — it gives real, nonzero sampling mass to tokens the model scored as implausible in the first place. Some of that tail is stylistic variety; a good chunk of it is the model confidently generating claims, facts, or citations it has no real support for, because the sampler handed a genuinely low-probability token a real chance to get picked. There's no free lunch here: the same mechanism that makes brainstorming output feel less robotic is what makes factual-extraction output start hallucinating specifics.

**Amateurs are baffled that the same prompt gives a different answer every time, or that hitting "regenerate" ever helps. Pros know that's the sampler working as designed, not a bug.** Every non-greedy decode is a weighted dice roll over the model's output distribution — unless something pins temperature to 0 and the hardware cooperates, the same prompt genuinely can and will produce different completions on different calls. That's not the model being unreliable; it's the entire point of using anything other than greedy decoding. And it's exactly why "regenerate" is a meaningful button rather than a placebo: it's another independent roll of the same dice, over the same distribution, and a bad roll on the first try doesn't mean the next one will be bad too.

**Amateurs use the same sampling settings for every task. Pros match the settings to what the task actually rewards.** Extraction, classification, and code generation have one correct-ish answer and heavily punish creative deviation — low temperature (near the deterministic end) and either top-p left at or near 1 or nudged down slightly is the right default, because you want the model's single best-supported guess, not an interesting one. Brainstorming, creative writing, and anything where "several different plausible answers" beats "one narrow answer" reward the opposite — a higher temperature, sometimes paired with a moderately restrictive top-p to keep the tail from getting *too* wild. The knob isn't a personality setting; it's a tradeoff between reliability and variety, and the right position depends entirely on which side of that tradeoff the task actually needs.
