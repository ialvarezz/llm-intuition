# 04 · Attention & Transformers

## The context problem

"Bank" means something different in "she sat on the river bank" and "she deposited a check at the bank." A tokenizer (note 02) turns both into the same token ID, and the embedding table (note 03) starts both off at the exact same vector — the model has no way, at that point, to know which "bank" it's looking at. Something has to happen after tokenization and embedding that lets a word's meaning shift based on the words around it. That something is **attention**, and it's the mechanism the whole modern LLM architecture is built around. As the 3Blue1Brown lesson on the topic puts it, "the aim of a transformer is to progressively adjust these embeddings so that they don't merely encode data of an individual word, but rather a much richer contextual meaning" [S3]. Attention is the step that does the adjusting.

The intuitive version: every token in a sequence asks a question — *which earlier tokens matter to me right now, and how much?* — then blends in information from whichever tokens answer that question strongly. For "it" in a sentence like "the robot picked up the ball because it was light," the token "it" needs to figure out that "ball" (not "robot") is what it's standing in for, before the model can predict what comes next correctly. Jay Alammar's Illustrated Transformer works through the same idea with a different sentence, "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired" [S2], noting that "when the model is processing the word 'it', self-attention allows it to associate 'it' with 'animal'" [S2]. That association isn't hard-coded — it's the result of every token comparing itself against every earlier token and deciding, numerically, how relevant each one is.

## Queries, keys, values

The mechanics of "which tokens matter to me" run through three vectors computed per token: **query**, **key**, and **value**. None of these are natural properties of a word — they're vectors "created by multiplying the embedding by three matrices that we trained during the training process" [S2], and Alammar is upfront that "they're abstractions that are useful for calculating and thinking about attention" [S2] rather than things with an independent meaning outside that calculation.

The plain-language version, worked through with the example sentence "A fluffy blue creature roamed the verdant forest" [S3]:

- **Query** — what a token is looking for. "We can imagine each noun in the sentence asking the question: 'Hey, are there any adjectives sitting in front of me?' These questions...are somehow encoded as yet another vector that we call the query" [S3]. In the toy sentence, the noun "creature" is effectively asking that question.
- **Key** — what a token advertises about itself, as a potential answer to other tokens' queries. "We want to think of these keys as potential answers to the queries...the key matrix maps the adjectives like fluffy and blue to vectors that are closely aligned with the query produced by the word creature" [S3]. A query and a key that point in a similar direction produce a high match score — concretely, "a larger dot product corresponds to stronger alignment" [S3] between the two vectors.
- **Value** — what a token actually hands over once it's been picked as relevant. "The value vector is something that would be added to the embedding of the second word...to move it to a different part of the dimensional embedding space" [S3]. The query/key comparison decides *how much* attention one token pays to another; the value is *what gets transferred* once that weight is set.

Put together: every token computes a query; every token also exposes a key; the model compares each token's query against every earlier token's key to get a set of match scores; those scores get turned into weights (via softmax, so they sum to something like 100%); and each token's new representation becomes a weighted blend of every earlier token's value, weighted by how well their key matched the query. This is genuinely one mechanism doing genuinely one job — no separate lookup table, no rules about grammar. It's a comparison ("does your key match my query?") and a weighted hand-off ("if so, take some of your value").

One consequence worth sitting with: this transfer isn't limited to neighboring words. "This transfer of information from the embedding of one token to that of another can occur over potentially large distances and can involve information that's much richer than just a single word" [S3] — a token at the end of a long paragraph can attend directly back to a token at the start of it, in one step, with no chain of intermediate words required to carry the information forward.

## Heads and layers

A single query/key/value comparison captures one *kind* of relationship at a time — say, "which earlier token does this pronoun refer to." Language needs many kinds of relationships tracked simultaneously: grammatical agreement, coreference, topical relevance, and more. Transformers handle this with **multi-head attention** — several independent query/key/value comparisons running in parallel within the same attention layer, each with its own learned weight matrices. The original paper frames the benefit directly: "multi-head attention allows the model to jointly attend to information from different representation subspaces at different positions. With a single attention head, averaging inhibits this" [S1]. Alammar's summary of the practical payoff is the same idea in plainer terms: multiple heads expand "the model's ability to focus on different positions" and give "the attention layer multiple 'representation subspaces'" [S2] — in practice, one head might end up specializing in something like syntactic agreement while another tracks which noun a pronoun refers to, without anyone designing that division of labor by hand; it falls out of training. Anthropic's interpretability research describes the underlying structure the same way: "each attention layer consists of multiple heads, which operate in parallel" [S4], and heads are "independent operations, each outputting a result which is added into the residual stream" [S4] — the heads don't coordinate explicitly; their outputs just get summed together.

A **transformer** stacks many of these attention layers on top of each other, and each one is paired with a small feed-forward network applied to every position. Alammar describes the building block directly: "each one is broken down into two sub-layers...the outputs of the self-attention layer are fed to a feed-forward neural network" [S2], and the original architecture "stacks six of them on top of each other" [S2] in the encoder and decoder — modern LLMs stack far more; Anthropic's own description of a large model notes that a network like GPT-3 has "96 layers and alternates attention blocks with MLP blocks" [S4]. Attention lets information move between token positions; the feed-forward layer processes each position's (now context-enriched) vector on its own. Stack enough of these pairs and the representation of each token gets progressively richer — moving from "raw token embedding" toward something that encodes the token's full role in its specific sentence.

One more architectural detail matters for anything that generates text one token at a time: **causal masking**. When a transformer predicts the next token, it isn't allowed to attend to tokens that haven't been generated yet — otherwise it would be "cheating" by looking at the answer. As the 3Blue1Brown lesson puts it, "the masking process is done to prevent later tokens from influencing earlier ones" [S3]. In practice, this means a token only ever attends backward, to itself and everything before it, never forward.

## Why transformers won

Before transformers, the dominant approach for sequence data was recurrent neural networks (RNNs), which process a sequence one step at a time — each step's computation depends on the previous step's output. The original transformer paper names exactly why that's a structural bottleneck: RNNs "generate a sequence of hidden states h_t, as a function of the previous hidden state h_{t-1} and the input for position t. This inherently sequential nature precludes parallelization within training examples" [S1]. You cannot compute step 500 of an RNN until you've computed step 499 — there's no way to hand different chunks of one sequence to different GPU cores at the same time.

Attention breaks that dependency. Every token's query/key/value comparison against every other token can, in principle, be computed simultaneously — it's a large matrix multiplication, exactly the kind of operation GPUs are built to do fast in parallel. The paper's own framing of the payoff: the resulting models are "superior in quality while being more parallelizable and requiring significantly less time to train" [S1] — its own version of the Transformer trained to a new state-of-the-art translation result "after training for 3.5 days on eight GPUs" [S1], a training time that would have been far longer for a comparably capable RNN. Better quality and faster training, from the same architectural change — that combination is why the transformer replaced RNNs as the default architecture for language models within a few years of the 2017 paper.

## Pro vs. amateur

**Amateurs assume longer context is "just" a bigger buffer. Pros know attention cost scales quadratically with sequence length.** The original paper's own complexity table gives self-attention a cost of "O(n² · d)" per layer, where n is sequence length and d is the representation dimension, versus O(n) sequential steps for a comparable recurrent layer [S1] — a self-attention layer "connects all positions with a constant number of sequentially executed operations, whereas a recurrent layer requires O(n) sequential operations" [S1]. That n² term is the direct explanation for why context windows have hard limits and why doubling the tokens in a prompt doesn't double the compute cost — it roughly quadruples the attention computation. Context limits aren't an arbitrary product decision; they're downstream of this one formula.

**Amateurs treat "the model attends to X" as a single, clean number. Pros know attention is per-head, per-layer, and there's no one attention pattern to point to.** A transformer with, say, 32 layers and multiple heads per layer produces a separate attention pattern for every head in every layer, all running in parallel — "each attention layer consists of multiple heads, which operate in parallel" [S4], and heads are "independent operations, each outputting a result which is added into the residual stream" [S4]. Asking "what does the model attend to when it reads this sentence" doesn't have a single answer; it has one answer per head, and those answers change from layer to layer as the representation gets progressively transformed.

**Amateurs read attention weights as an explanation of model behavior. Pros treat them as one ingredient in a much larger computation.** It's tempting to visualize an attention heatmap and conclude "the model is basing its output on this token" — but Anthropic's own interpretability research is explicit that a clean, human-readable account of what a head is doing only holds "if one freezes the attention patterns" [S4] and separately accounts for what happens to the values that get moved — attention weights on their own aren't a self-contained explanation, because the query/key/value transformation, the feed-forward layers, and every other head and layer in the stack are all contributing to the final output too. A high attention weight tells you information moved from one token to another; it doesn't by itself tell you why that mattered to the model's eventual prediction.

**Amateurs assume a transformer inherently knows word order. Pros know attention itself is order-blind, and positional encoding is a bolt-on fix.** The query/key/value comparison described above treats the input as an unordered set of vectors — nothing in the mechanism itself distinguishes "the dog bit the man" from "the man bit the dog" by position alone. The original paper is direct about why this needed a separate fix: "since our model contains no recurrence and no convolution, in order for the model to make use of the order of the sequence, we must inject some information about the relative or absolute position of the tokens in the sequence" [S1]. Alammar describes the practical version of the same fix: "the transformer adds a vector to each input embedding...helps it determine the position of each word" [S2]. Word order isn't a free property of the architecture — it's information that has to be deliberately injected before attention runs, because attention on its own would happily ignore it.
