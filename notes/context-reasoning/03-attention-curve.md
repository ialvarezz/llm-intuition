# 03 · Attention & the attention curve

Module 00 introduced attention as the mechanism that lets a model weigh other tokens when building the representation of a given token — the reason "it" in a sentence can end up meaning "animal" rather than "street," despite the two words being nowhere near each other in the raw text. Concept 02 of this module then established that a context window is a working-memory budget with composition, not just a size limit: what's in the window, how much is redundant, and how relevant the mix is all shape output quality before the token cap is ever hit. This note connects the two: attention isn't a uniform lookup across everything in that window. Where a piece of information sits changes how much weight it actually gets — a distinct lever from what's in the window at all.

## Recap: attention is a weighting, not a lookup

The mental model worth carrying over from Module 00 is that attention computes a *weighted combination*, not a database read. For each token, the model asks — in the Illustrated Transformer's plain-language framing — a Query, matched against every other token's Key, producing a Value-weighted blend of everyone else's representation [S2]. Vaswani et al.'s original formulation calls this "an attention mechanism relating different positions of a single sequence in order to compute a representation of the sequence" [S3]. Crucially, *every* token gets some weight in that computation — attention doesn't select one relevant token and ignore the rest, it distributes a limited amount of "attention mass" across all of them, with the model choosing (via training) how unevenly to distribute it.

That framing already implies the concept for this note: if attention mass is a fixed budget spread across every token in the context, then adding more tokens to attend over doesn't just cost compute — the O(n²) pairwise-relationship cost noted in the original paper [S3] and reconfirmed by Anthropic's engineering team [S4] — it also means each individual token gets, on average, a smaller slice of that budget. A ten-token context and a ten-thousand-token context are not just different in size; they're different in how much attention any single token in them can expect to receive. But "on average" hides the real story, because the *distribution* isn't even across position either. Some positions reliably get more attention than their content alone would justify, and some get systematically less. That unevenness is the attention curve, and it's a second, independent lever from the ones concept 02 already covered.

## Lost in the middle

The clearest empirical demonstration of the curve comes from Liu et al.'s 2023 study, which tested how well language models retrieve a piece of relevant information depending on where in a long input context that information was placed. The finding, stated in the paper's own words: "performance is often highest when relevant information occurs at the beginning or end of the input context, and significantly degrades when models must access relevant information in the middle of long contexts, even for explicitly long-context models" [S1]. Read that last clause carefully — "even for explicitly long-context models" is the detail that rules out the easy explanation. This isn't a limitation that only shows up in models with small context windows straining past their comfortable range; it holds for models specifically built and marketed for handling long inputs. A bigger advertised context window does not fix this. It's a separate axis of degradation from raw capacity.

The shape this produces, plotted as recall (or task accuracy) against position in the context, is the U-curve this concept is named for: high near the start, high again near the end, and a trough somewhere in the middle. It's worth being precise about what kind of finding this is — it's a *behavioral* result, measured by testing whether the model's output correctly used information placed at a given position, not a direct measurement of attention weights. That distinction matters for the next section, because a second, independent line of work closes that gap.

A 2024 follow-up, Yu et al.'s "Found in the Middle," set out specifically to understand *why* the lost-in-the-middle behavior happens, and found a matching pattern at the mechanism level: "LLMs exhibit a U-shaped attention bias where the tokens at the beginning and at the end of its input receive higher attention, regardless of their relevance" [S5]. The phrase "regardless of their relevance" is the important qualifier — this isn't the model correctly judging that early and late tokens happen to matter more for a given task; it's a positional bias baked into how attention gets allocated, independent of content. Two different methodologies — one measuring task accuracy by position (Liu et al.), one measuring raw attention weights by position (Yu et al.) — landing on the same U-shape is good reason to treat the curve as a real, replicated property of how these models process long context, not an artifact of one particular evaluation design.

## Why this happens (mechanically)

None of the sources in this dump claim to fully explain, at the level of training dynamics, why the U-shape emerges — that remains an active research question. But the pieces that are well-established point at a plausible mechanical story, built from what's already established about how attention and autoregressive generation work.

**Primacy: strong early conditioning.** In an autoregressive, causally-masked model, every token generated later in the sequence is conditioned on everything before it. The masking is deliberate: the original transformer paper modifies self-attention in the decoder "to prevent positions from attending to subsequent positions," so that "the predictions for position i can depend only on the known outputs at positions less than i" [S3]. The very first tokens in a context set up the frame the entire rest of the generation happens inside; they are, structurally, present in the conditioning context for every single token that follows, from token 2 all the way to the end. That gives early content an outsized, persistent role that later content, even highly relevant later content, structurally cannot replicate — nothing placed at position 5,000 gets to be "present" for the interpretation of positions 1 through 4,999 the way position 1 is present for everything after it.

**Recency: proximity in the causal mask.** At the opposite end, the tokens immediately preceding the point of generation dominate local attention patterns simply because they're close. Local coherence — grammar, immediate topic continuity, the last few things that were said — depends heavily on nearby tokens, and a model trained to produce locally fluent text learns to weight nearby context heavily as a matter of course. The end of a long context is, by definition, always "nearby" to whatever comes right after it.

**The middle: diluted by competition.** Content sitting far from both the start and the point of generation benefits from neither effect — it isn't part of the persistent early frame, and it isn't close enough to dominate local attention. It also has the most competing tokens on both sides bidding for a share of the fixed attention budget described above. With attention mass spread across many tokens at that distance, and no positional advantage pulling weight toward it the way primacy and recency do, middle content ends up systematically diluted — present in the window, technically available, but underweighted relative to its actual relevance. This is exactly the "regardless of their relevance" bias Yu et al. measured directly [S5].

## This compounds with concept 02

Concept 02 established that a context window's *composition* — what's in it, how redundant it is, how relevant the mix is — matters independently of whether the token cap has been reached. This concept adds a second, independent lever: *position* matters too, independently of composition. A window can be perfectly curated — no redundancy, everything in it genuinely relevant to the task — and still underperform if the single most important instruction happens to land in the geometric middle of a long document dump. Composition answers "is the right information in the window at all?" Position answers a different question: "given that it's in there, does the model actually weight it appropriately?" Getting composition right is necessary but not sufficient; a well-curated window with badly-placed critical content is still a window that underuses its best material.

This is also the sharper, mechanistic version of the "context rot" pattern from concept 02: Anthropic's framing that "as the number of tokens in the context window increases, the model's ability to accurately recall information from that context decreases" [S4] describes an aggregate trend across window size. The attention curve explains part of *why*: it isn't that every token degrades uniformly as the window grows, it's that the tokens sitting in the vulnerable middle band degrade the most, while start and end positions hold up comparatively well even as the window fills. "Longer context, worse recall on average" and "recall depends heavily on where in the context something sits" are two faces of the same underlying phenomenon.

## Practical implications

A few concrete habits follow directly from the shape of the curve:

- **Don't bury the critical instruction in the middle of a long document dump.** If a prompt includes a large block of reference material — retrieved documents, a long codebase excerpt, a transcript — the instruction that actually governs what to do with that material should sit at the start, the end, or both, not somewhere in the middle of the pasted content.
- **Repeat critical constraints near the end of a long prompt.** Because recency gives the tail of the context outsized weight, restating a hard constraint right before the model is asked to respond costs a small number of tokens and buys back some of the primacy/recency advantage that a constraint stated only once, far earlier, may have lost by the time generation happens.
- **Order retrieved chunks by importance, not by original document order.** When assembling retrieved passages into a prompt (RAG-style), defaulting to the source document's original ordering is arbitrary with respect to the attention curve. Placing the highest-relevance chunk first or last — rather than wherever it happened to fall in the source — puts it in the position most likely to actually get used.

## Pro vs. amateur

Amateurs see "128k context" or "1M context" in a model's marketing and treat that number as a promise of uniform attention quality across the whole window — if it fits, the model will use it equally well no matter where it sits. Pros know the number is a capacity ceiling, not a quality guarantee, and that recall varies substantially by position even well inside that ceiling, including on models explicitly built for long context [S1].

Amateurs state a critical constraint once, early in the prompt, and trust the model to carry it forward through everything that follows. Pros know that a single early statement is vulnerable to exactly the middle-dilution and end-of-context recency effects described above once enough material piles up after it, and so they restate load-bearing constraints — especially right before the point where the model has to act on them.

Amateurs paste retrieved or injected content in whatever order it arrived in — file order, search-result order, chronological order — because that ordering is convenient to produce. Pros order deliberately: most-important-first-or-last, because the curve rewards the edges of the window and punishes the middle regardless of how relevant the content genuinely is [S5].
