How Models Are Trained

A frontier model is built in stages: a vast autocomplete engine is grown first, then taught to follow instructions, then tuned toward what people actually prefer. Each stage leaves fingerprints you can see in every response.

Pretraining: growing an autocomplete engine

Before a model can be an assistant, it has to become something much dumber and much more general: a next-token predictor. Pretraining takes a raw architecture and trillions of tokens of text — web pages, books, code, forum posts — and trains it on a single objective, over and over: given everything so far, predict the next token. Nothing about being helpful is in that objective. Nothing about answering questions, following instructions, or refusing bad requests is in it either. The only signal is whether it guessed the next token correctly.

What's striking is how much falls out of that one objective, given enough scale. To get good at predicting the next token across a huge, varied corpus, the model has to implicitly learn grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, code syntax, argument structure — because all of that shows up in the statistics of what token comes next. Loss goes down over the course of pretraining, and as it does, capabilities that were never directly trained for start to emerge as side effects of getting better at the underlying prediction task. This is the stage that costs the most, by a wide margin, and it's also where the data gets locked in — Meta describes building "our dataset for language model pre-training from a variety of data sources containing knowledge until the end of 2023", a boundary set at the data-collection stage, well before any fine-tuning happens.

The thing that comes out the other end of pretraining is a base model, and it's important to be precise about what that is: a very good, very general document-completion engine, not an assistant. Feed it The capital of France is and it will complete the sentence. Feed it a question, and depending on what similar text looked like in its training data, it might answer, might continue the question with more questions, might drift into a forum-post-style tangent, or might just keep generating similar-looking text without ever answering the way a chatbot would. A base model has no built-in concept of being the assistant half of a conversation — that framing gets taught later.

Supervised fine-tuning (SFT): teaching the format

A base model can technically produce a correct answer to explain what a token is, but it has no learned habit of doing so cleanly, and no reason to prefer that over rambling into related trivia. Supervised fine-tuning fixes that by training the model on a much smaller, much higher-quality dataset: thousands of curated (instruction → good response) pairs, written or approved by human labelers, that demonstrate what a well-formed helpful answer actually looks like. Where pretraining predicts the next token over a slice of the internet, SFT predicts the next token over a curated set of examples that all look like a helpful assistant talking.

The InstructGPT paper — the source that popularized this exact pipeline for the assistant models most people now use — describes this first step plainly: "Our labelers provide demonstrations of the desired behavior on the input prompt distribution", and that demonstration dataset is what the base model gets fine-tuned on using ordinary supervised learning. It's a much smaller, much cheaper stage than pretraining, but it's the one that does the most obvious work of turning a document-completer into something that looks and acts like a chat assistant: answering the question that was actually asked, in a recognizable format, instead of wandering off into whatever a similar-looking passage on the internet would have done next.

RLHF: optimizing toward what people actually prefer

SFT teaches the model to imitate good responses, but imitation caps out: the model can only be as good as the examples it was shown, and there's no mechanism in SFT for it to improve past what a human demonstrator happened to write down. Reinforcement learning from human feedback adds a second, different kind of signal on top: instead of imitating one fixed correct answer, the model learns to produce whichever answer humans prefer out of several candidates, even when no human ever wrote that exact answer down.

The mechanism, per InstructGPT, runs in two more steps after SFT. First: "We collect a dataset of comparisons between model outputs, where labelers indicate which output they prefer", and that comparison data trains a separate reward model — a model whose entire job is to look at a response and predict how much a human labeler would like it. Second, the SFT model gets optimized against that reward model: "We use the output of the RM as a scalar reward. We fine-tune the supervised policy to optimize this reward using the PPO algorithm." The payoff was large: InstructGPT's paper reports that "labelers significantly prefer InstructGPT outputs across model sizes", with a 1.3-billion-parameter InstructGPT model beating the 175-billion-parameter base GPT-3 in human evaluations — roughly 100x fewer parameters, still preferred.

Anthropic's Constitutional AI paper describes a variant of this same preference-optimization stage that swaps out the human ranking step. In the supervised phase, a model critiques and revises its own response against a written principle: "we first generate responses to harmfulness prompts using a helpful-only AI assistant... We then ask the model to critique its response according to a principle in the constitution, and then revise the original response in light of the critique." In the RL phase, another model ranks the candidates instead of a human: "This stage mimics RLHF, except that we replace human preferences for harmlessness with 'AI feedback'" — the paper calls this "RL from AI Feedback" (RLAIF). The paper is explicit that the goal is reducing dependence on RLHF, not replacing the whole idea: Constitutional AI "improves upon, and partially replaces reinforcement learning from human feedback" by relying on written principles instead of "tens of thousands of human preference labels".

It's worth being explicit about why this is several separate stages instead of one. Pretraining builds capability — knowledge, language ability, reasoning, everything the model could possibly say comes from here. SFT teaches format — installing the habit of presenting an answer cleanly, on-topic, in assistant style. RLHF (or RLAIF) aligns toward preference — pushing the model toward whatever real evaluators consistently rate higher, generalizing past the specific examples any human wrote by hand. Put together, this is also where a model's personality comes from: not from pretraining, which just produces raw capability, but from the two post-training stages layered on top. Tone, hedging habits, refusal patterns, formality — all downstream of what the SFT examples looked like and what the reward model was trained to prefer.

Pro vs. amateur

Amateurs assume a model's knowledge cutoff tracks its release or deployment date. Pros know it tracks the pretraining data. A model announced in a given month can still have a knowledge cutoff many months earlier, because the pretraining corpus was assembled and frozen well before training even started, and none of the post-training stages add new world knowledge at any real scale. Meta's own description of Llama 3's pretraining data — "knowledge until the end of 2023" — sets that boundary at data collection, long before the model that eventually ships to users.

Amateurs think a model refuses or over-apologizes because it understood the request was bad. Pros know refusal behavior is a trained preference, not comprehension. Refusals, hedging, and excessive caveats aren't the model reasoning its way to caution — they're patterns reinforced because a reward model, trained on human or AI preference data, consistently scored that kind of response higher for that kind of prompt. A model that over-refuses isn't being extra thoughtful; it's exhibiting a training artifact, and different post-training choices produce visibly different refusal behavior on the same underlying base model.

Amateurs try to teach a model new facts by fine-tuning it. Pros know fine-tuning shapes behavior, not knowledge. SFT and RLHF/RLAIF are about how the model expresses what it already has some representation of, not about injecting new, reliable factual content. Fine-tuning datasets — thousands of examples — are nowhere near the scale of pretraining's trillions of tokens, so they're a poor tool for reliably teaching new facts; a model may memorize a fact from a fine-tuning set inconsistently, or not at all, or generalize it incorrectly. That's precisely the gap retrieval-augmented approaches exist to fill — put the facts in context at inference time instead of trying to bake them into weights after the fact.

Amateurs think the assistant persona is the whole model. Pros know a base model is still in there, underneath. SFT and RLHF reshape how the model responds, but they don't erase the underlying next-token-prediction machinery pretraining built — they bias it. Push a deployed model with an unusual prompt structure, a broken conversation format, or an edge case the post-training data didn't cover well, and it can lapse back toward raw-autocomplete behavior: rambling, drifting off-topic, completing a pattern instead of answering a question. That's the base model's document-completion habit showing through the assistant veneer, not a new failure mode appearing from nowhere.
